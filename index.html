09
Make the Most

An internship is not just a checkbox — it is your first real step into your career. It signals to every future employer that you can function in a professional environment, contribute to real projects, and learn on the job. Whether you are in your 1st year or final year, this guide walks you through exactly what to do.

💡  Every step is designed to be immediately actionable. Don't just read — do.
01
Understand What Employers Actually Want
Start with clarity, not guesswork

Before you build anything — a resume, a GitHub, a portfolio — understand the lens through which companies evaluate interns. They are not looking for perfection. They are looking for potential, attitude, and basic competence.

🧠

Problem-Solving Ability

Can you break down a problem and think through solutions logically? This matters more than memorising syntax.

🤝

Communication Skills

Can you explain your work clearly? Can you ask good questions? Employers value clarity as much as technical skill.

🔧

Relevant Technical Basics

You don't need to know everything — just enough of the domain to hit the ground running with guidance.

📈

Willingness to Learn

Interns who ask great questions and adapt fast are far more valuable than those who pretend to know everything.

Companies hire interns to build a pipeline of future employees. They want someone who fits their culture and can grow with them — not a finished product.

— Key Insight

03
Craft a Standout Resume
One page. Zero fluff. Maximum impact.

Your resume is a marketing document, not a biography. Recruiters spend an average of 6–7 seconds on an initial scan. Every word must earn its place.

Rule 1

Keep it to One Page

Unless you have 5+ years of experience, one page is the standard. Prioritise ruthlessly — if it doesn't add value, cut it.

Rule 2

Use the STAR Format

Situation → Task → Action → Result. Instead of "Worked on a website", write: "Built a responsive e-commerce frontend using React that reduced page load time by 40%."

Rule 3

Lead with Projects

If you lack work experience, a strong Projects section is your best asset. List 2–3 real projects with tech stack, your role, and measurable outcome.

Rule 4

Use an ATS-Friendly Template

Applicant Tracking Systems scan resumes. Avoid tables, graphics, and fancy columns. Use plain fonts. Try Resumake.io, Overleaf, or Canva (simple template).

Rule 5

Tailor for Each Application

Mirror keywords from the job description. If they say "Python and data visualisation", make sure those exact terms appear in your resume.

Essential Sections to Include:

  • Contact Info — Name, email, phone, LinkedIn, GitHub/Portfolio URL
  • Education — College, branch, CGPA (include only if ≥ 7.0)
  • Skills — Technical skills grouped by category
  • Projects — 2–4 with tech stack, description, and outcomes
  • Experience — Internships, part-time work, research (if any)
  • Achievements — Hackathon wins, coding ranks, certifications
  • Extra-curricular — Leadership roles, clubs (be selective)
04
Build Your Online Presence
Be findable before you even apply

Most recruiters and hiring managers will search your name online before deciding to call you. Make sure what they find tells a compelling story.

💼

LinkedIn Profile

Professional photo. Headline beyond "Student" — e.g., "Aspiring ML Engineer | Python | TensorFlow | 2 projects". Fill the About section. Connect with 50+ people.

💻

GitHub Profile

Pin your best 4–6 repos. Write clear README files for each project — what it does, how to run it, what you learned. Even 3 solid projects is enough.

🌐

Portfolio Website

Optional but powerful. A simple single-page site with your projects, skills, and contact info. Use GitHub Pages, Vercel, or Netlify — all free.

🏆

Competitive Coding

Active LeetCode / Codeforces / HackerRank profiles signal problem-solving ability. Aim for 50+ solved problems on LeetCode if targeting software roles.

🔗 Your LinkedIn URL should be customised (e.g., linkedin.com/in/yourname) — not the default random string. It takes 30 seconds in settings and looks far more professional.
05
Find & Target the Right Opportunities
Know where to look and what to look for

Most students apply only to popular portals and miss the best opportunities. Cast a wider net and use a smarter strategy.

Best Platforms:

LinkedIn JobsInternshalaUnstop AngelList / WellfoundNaukriHackerEarth Company Career PagesIndeedGlassdoor InstahyreIIT / IISc Research Portals
Strategy 1

Cold Email Startups Directly

Startups rarely post openings but almost always need help. Find 20–30 startups in your domain on LinkedIn or AngelList, find the founder's email, and send a short, personalised email offering to contribute.

Strategy 2

Apply to Research Internships

Many students ignore research internships — which is why they're easier to get. IISER, IIT, and central university research labs offer stipended internships (SURGE, SRF, IUAC). Great for core branch students.

Strategy 3

Use Your College's Alumni Network

Seniors who graduated 2–5 years ago are your single best resource. They know who's hiring, what interviews look like, and will often refer you directly.

Strategy 4

Participate in Hackathons & Open Source

Winning or doing well in hackathons (Devfolio, Unstop, MLH) leads directly to internship offers. Contributing to open-source on GitHub catches recruiter attention and builds your portfolio simultaneously.

06
Network Strategically
Relationships open doors that applications can't

Networking is not about being fake or transactional. It is about building genuine relationships with people doing work you respect. Most internships — especially off-campus ones — come through a connection, not a cold application.

Step 1

Connect with Seniors and Alumni

Message 2–3 seniors per week on LinkedIn. Keep it short: "Hi [Name], I'm a 2nd-year CSE student at [College]. I noticed you're at [Company] — I'm exploring internships in [domain] and would love 10 minutes of your advice."

Step 2

Engage on LinkedIn and Twitter/X

Comment thoughtfully on posts by people in your field. Share your own projects or learnings. A recruiter who has seen your name 3 times is far more likely to respond to your application.

Step 3

Attend Tech Events, Meetups, and Webinars

GDG meetups, PyData, local hackathons, college tech fests — these are where you meet professionals informally. Follow up on LinkedIn within 24 hours.

Step 4

Ask for Referrals (the right way)

Only ask for a referral after you've built rapport. Never ask a stranger to refer you — instead, ask for insights about the role, and let referrals come naturally.

The best internship leads often come from a professor, a senior you helped, or someone you met at a hackathon — not from a job portal. Start building relationships now, not when you need them.

— Networking Principle
07
Apply Smart, Not Just Hard
Quality and consistency beat volume alone

Blasting your resume to 200 companies without customisation is a waste of time. A focused, tailored approach with consistent follow-up delivers far better results.

🎯

Target 10–15 Companies Per Week

Research each company. Understand their product and tech stack. Write a personalised cover note for each. Quality gets more replies than mass applying.

✉️

Write a Sharp Cover Note

3–5 sentences: Why this company? What specific value do you bring? What's one thing you've built that's relevant? Keep it human, not template-like.

📊

Track Every Application

Use a spreadsheet: Company | Role | Date Applied | Status | Follow-up Date. Following up after 7–10 days doubles your response rate.

⏱️

Apply Early in the Cycle

Many postings close early once they get enough applications. Apply within 48–72 hours of a posting going live. Set job alerts for your target keywords.

📬 The Follow-Up Formula: Wait 7–10 days after applying, then send a polite 2-line follow-up email. "Hi [Name], I applied for the [Role] internship on [Date] and remain very interested. I'd love to know if there's any additional information I can provide." — Simple, respectful, effective.
08
Ace the Interview
Preparation + Confidence + Honesty

Internship interviews are generally less intense than full-time interviews, but preparation is still non-negotiable. Here's what to expect and how to prepare:

TECHNICAL PREPARATION

  • Revise all projects on your resume — expect deep questions
  • Practice 30–50 LeetCode Easy/Medium problems (for CS roles)
  • Know OOPs, OS, DBMS, CN cold (CS students)
  • Be ready to code on a shared screen or whiteboard
  • Review the company's tech stack and products

BEHAVIOURAL PREPARATION

  • Prepare your "Tell me about yourself" — under 90 seconds
  • Use STAR format for challenges faced, teamwork, failures
  • Have 3 questions ready to ask the interviewer
  • Research the company's mission, recent news, and culture
  • Practice out loud — do at least 3 mock interviews
On the Day

The Day-of Checklist

Test your internet and camera (online interviews). Dress professionally even for video calls. Keep your resume, a notepad, and water nearby. Join 5 minutes early. If you don't know the answer, say so clearly and walk through your thinking — interviewers value honesty over false confidence.

❌ Never lie about your resume. If you listed a skill, you will be asked about it. If you listed a project, expect questions on every design decision. Only put things on your resume that you can speak about confidently.
09
Make the Most of Your Internship
Getting the offer is just the beginning

Many students treat the internship as a finish line. It is actually a starting line. How you perform here directly shapes your full-time offers, references, and professional reputation.

Week 1–2

Observe, Listen, and Ask Smart Questions

Don't rush to prove yourself. Understand the team's workflow, tools, codebase, and communication style. Ask questions that show you've done your homework.

Weeks 3–6

Deliver Quality Work Consistently

Meet every deadline. Document your work clearly. When you encounter a blocker, try for at least 30 minutes yourself before asking — and present what you've already tried. Reliability is the most underrated intern skill.

Throughout

Build Real Relationships

Have brief, genuine conversations with team members beyond your direct work. Attend team meetings actively. These relationships lead to references, PPOs, and future collaborations.

Final Week

Leave a Strong Impression

Present your work clearly and completely. Write thorough handover notes. Ask for a LinkedIn recommendation from your manager. Request honest feedback. Thank the team personally.
